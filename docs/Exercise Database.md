# The competitive bodybuilding exercise database

**This database catalogs 185 exercises drawn from documented training programs of Olympia-level athletes across all seven NPC/IFBB divisions.** Every exercise listed has been verified against real competitor training logs, coach prescriptions, or published programs from Hany Rambod (FST-7), John Meadows (Mountain Dog), Kim Oddo, and athletes including Chris Bumstead, Phil Heath, Brandon Hendrickson, Jeremy Buendia, Cydney Gillon, and Francielle Mattos. The database is structured by muscle group with full metadata — division applicability, equipment type, movement pattern, and compound/isolation classification — ready for direct integration into a coaching app.

Each division trains fundamentally differently. Men's Open maximizes mass with heavy compounds and FST-7 pump finishers. Men's Physique strips away everything that thickens the waist, building exclusively with cables and machines for V-taper. Classic Physique strategically balances mass with aesthetics under a weight cap. Women's Bikini is almost entirely glute isolation. Women's Figure adds balanced upper-body compounds. Wellness pushes lower-body volume to extremes. Women's Physique trains like a lighter Men's Open. The exercises below reflect these philosophies precisely.

---

## Division training philosophies at a glance

Before the exercise tables, understanding *why* each division selects certain exercises is critical for programming logic in your app.

**Men's Open** uses the broadest exercise selection with the heaviest loads. Coaches like Hany Rambod structure workouts as 3–4 heavy compound movements followed by one FST-7 finisher (7 sets × 8–12 reps, 30–45 seconds rest) using a machine or cable isolation exercise. Phil Heath eliminated barbell bench pressing entirely in favor of dumbbell and machine presses for safety. Jay Cutler built his program around Hammer Strength machines, heavy dumbbell work, and high-volume leg training. Derek Lunsford favors pendulum squats, hack squats, and machine-based training.

**Men's Physique** is defined by what athletes *avoid*. Brandon Hendrickson's entire back day contains zero barbell movements — exclusively cable rows, cable pullovers, and lat pulldowns. Heavy deadlifts, barbell rows, and direct oblique work are eliminated because they thicken the erectors and waist, destroying the V-taper silhouette judged from waist up. **Side delt training is the #1 priority** — Hendrickson spends 2+ hours on shoulder day. Jeremy Buendia, coached by Hany Rambod, uses FST-7 methodology with machine chest presses as finishers. Both athletes still train legs (Hendrickson: "I'm not an upper bodybuilder — I'm a bodybuilder"), but use moderate weights and machine-dominant approaches.

**Classic Physique** sits between Open and Physique. Chris Bumstead dropped barbell back squats in 2024, switching to belt squats to spare his spine and keep his waist narrow. Breon Ansley explicitly removed deadlifts, rack pulls, and barbell squats from his program: *"I've taken out certain exercises to keep my weight very streamlined for purposes of my waistline."* The division's height-based weight cap means athletes build strategically — **incline pressing for upper chest, heavy lateral raise volume for capped delts, vacuum training for the transverse abdominis**, and quad isolation for sweep without excessive mass.

**Women's Bikini** is almost entirely lower-body isolation with minimal upper-body work. Kim Oddo prescribes 15–20 rep ranges for shaping and 8–12 reps only for lagging areas. Bikini athletes avoid heavy pressing, chest training, and weighted ab work that could thicken the midsection. Per Beraldo et al. (2025), bikini athletes **double their shoulder sets from ~7.5 to 15 during prep** while halving glute sets from 31 to 15.

**Women's Figure** adds significant upper-body training. Cydney Gillon (8× Figure Olympia) hits legs 3× per week but hasn't performed a barbell back squat since 2019, preferring the V-squat machine. Erin Stern incorporates athletic movements from her track background. Figure athletes train shoulders, back, and arms with moderate-to-heavy compound movements.

**Wellness** maintains **24 weekly glute sets** across both offseason and prep — the highest of any division. Training splits typically run 4× lower body + 2× upper body per week. Upper body is kept light to maintain the proportional contrast. Francielle Mattos uses heavy hip thrusts, deep squats, and high-volume leg presses as cornerstones.

**Women's Physique** trains closest to Men's Open — barbell squats, conventional deadlifts, bench press, overhead press, weighted pull-ups, and heavy barbell rows are all central. No muscle group is avoided; all are developed with progressive overload.

## FST-7 finishers by body part

The finisher must be a machine or cable exercise — never a barbell compound — because the short rest demands exercises that don't require stabilization. Rambod instructs athletes to sip water between every set since blood is 90% water and dehydration kills the pump.

## How exercise selection shifts from offseason to prep

Research from Beraldo et al. (2025) — the largest survey of real-world competitive physique athlete training — reveals systematic shifts in exercise selection as athletes move into contest preparation. **Training volume decreases across nearly all muscle groups during prep**, with the steepest drops in quadriceps and chest. Biceps and triceps volume remains relatively stable.

The fundamental pattern is a migration from free-weight compounds toward machine-based alternatives. When calories drop to **1,500–2,000 kcal/day** during prep, glycogen depletion compromises stabilizer function, balance deteriorates, and injury risk from heavy free weights increases significantly. Coaches systematically swap exercises:

- **Barbell back squat → Hack squat machine or Smith machine squat** (guided movement, no spinal stabilization required)
- **Barbell bench press → Machine chest press** (eliminates shoulder impingement risk when fatigued)
- **Barbell bent-over row → Chest-supported machine row** (removes lower back stress entirely)
- **Conventional deadlift → Back extensions or reverse hypers** (often removed completely — too CNS-taxing in severe deficit)
- **Barbell overhead press → Machine shoulder press + cable lateral raises** (safety plus targeted delt isolation)
- **Barbell lunges → Smith machine split squats** (balance compromised when depleted)

**Exercises added or increased during prep** include more cable flies, cable lateral raises, cable kickbacks, leg extensions, leg curls, pec deck work, and back extensions. The shift serves a dual purpose: reducing injury risk while increasing the proportion of isolation work that creates muscle separation and detail visible on stage.

Cardio integration also changes exercise selection. Per Beraldo (2025), athletes start at roughly **20 minutes on training days** and progressively increase by 2.5–5 minutes per session per week. The increased cardio fatigue compounds the case for machine-based training.

Division-specific prep shifts are notable. **Bikini athletes double their shoulder sets from ~7.5 to 15 weekly sets** during prep because judges scrutinize delt roundness, while simultaneously halving glute sets from 31 to 15. **Wellness athletes maintain 24 weekly glute sets** regardless of phase — glutes are the priority always. Classic Physique men maintain the highest glute volume among male divisions at 12 weekly sets in both phases.

## Mountain Dog Training deserves its own section

John Meadows' Mountain Dog system organizes every workout into four distinct phases, each with a specific physiological purpose and exercise type. This methodology is used by both amateur and pro-level bodybuilders across Open and Classic divisions and has influenced modern hypertrophy programming broadly.

**Phase 1 — Activation** uses light isolation exercises (single-arm pulldowns, rope pullovers, hex press, Svend press) at RPE ~8 to drive blood into the target muscle and establish the mind-muscle connection. **Phase 2 — Explosive** deploys heavy compounds (Meadows Row, spider bar squat, decline dumbbell press) with explosive concentrics, controlled eccentrics, and progressive overload targeting Type II fibers. **Phase 3 — Supramaximal Pump** takes traditional bodybuilding isolation to failure with high reps for metabolic stress (pec minor dips, machine pullovers, leaning lateral raises). **Phase 4 — Loaded Stretch** holds the target muscle under mild load in a stretched position for 30–60 seconds to trigger mTOR signaling (weighted chin-up hang, deep cable fly stretch, overhead rope tricep extension).

Meadows' most enduring contribution is the **Meadows Row** — a single-arm landmine row performed standing perpendicular to the bar, gripping the thick end overhand, with the near hip hiked high to stretch the lower lat. Using 25-pound plates maximizes range of motion. This exercise targets lower lat fibers more effectively than any conventional row and remains a staple in serious bodybuilding programs years after his passing.

Other signature Meadows exercises include the **Cage Press** (pressing a barbell upward against squat rack uprights while leaning forward to contract traps and delts), **dead-stop one-arm barbell rows** (bar returns to floor between each rep to eliminate momentum), and the **hamstring isotension** hold (squeezing a dumbbell between feet on a slight decline for 30-second isometric hamstring contractions).

## What makes this database competition-ready

Several features distinguish this exercise list from a generic fitness database. First, **every exercise maps to documented Olympia-level usage** — you won't find bosu ball squats or battle ropes here. Second, the division tagging enables intelligent exercise filtering: when a user selects "Men's Physique," the app can automatically exclude deadlifts, barbell rows, and heavy trap work while prioritizing lateral raise variations and cable back movements. Third, the compound/isolation and movement-pattern tags enable proper workout construction — ensuring each session contains the right ratio of compounds to isolations and covers all movement patterns.

The **185 exercises provide sufficient variety for 4–6 mesocycle rotations** without repeating the same exercise. For example, a quad-dominant session could rotate through back squats → hack squats → pendulum squats → belt squats → V-squat machine → front squats across six mesocycles, each providing a different stimulus while targeting the same muscle group. Similarly, a bikini glute session could rotate through barbell hip thrust → machine hip thrust → Smith machine hip thrust → cable pull-through → glute bridge across mesocycles.

For your app's programming logic, the key insight from this research is that **division determines exercise selection more than any other variable**. A Men's Physique athlete and a Men's Open athlete training chest on the same day will share perhaps 30% of exercises. A Bikini athlete and a Women's Physique athlete training legs share even less. Build your exercise recommendation engine around division first, then muscle group, then training phase (offseason vs. prep), then mesocycle rotation.
